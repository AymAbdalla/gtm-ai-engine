# One account, two buyers, two supreme laws

The same fictional account — Meridian Robotics, from the [account plan](account-plan-meridian-robotics.md) — with Day-1 emails to two different committee seats. This is the persona router doing its job: same triggers, same research, **different supreme law per buyer**, so different subject, structure, tone, and proof framing.

> **FICTIONAL EXAMPLE.** Companies, people, and figures are invented. In production, every number in an email must trace to a verified-stats file (a hard checklist gate, zero exceptions); the figures below are illustrative stand-ins for that mechanism.

---

## Email 1 — Chief People Officer (router → HR protocol, exec altitude)

```
Subject: Three Rosters

Mara,

Six weeks in, with three acquisitions on the books.
I'd guess three versions of every people process too.

Most teams hit this wall on PeopleNest.
It ran fine at 600 people. Not at 1,400 across nine countries.

Vantiv Labs merged three rosters onto one platform in a quarter.

Worth a look from whoever owns people systems?
```

**Why it's built this way:**

- **Router:** HR department + exec seniority → HR protocol supreme, executive altitude. Company-level observation, people-first framing — no feature language anywhere.
- **Opener** is two verifiable signals (tenure, acquisitions) fused into a problem she's living, not a compliment or a pitch.
- **Warmth + altitude:** conversational tone (the HR throughline), but the problem is org-level — this email would be wrong for her People Ops manager, and the router would never send it there.
- **One proof, band-matched:** a same-industry, similar-size customer, one line, no stacking.
- **The CTA is a delegate path** ("whoever owns people systems") — the exec-tier rule. The reply we want is a routing, not a meeting.
- **Craft law:** 57 words. Subject is 2 words, internal-sounding. One sentence per line for mobile. Zero em dashes. Ends on one low-friction question.

## Email 2 — CFO (router → Finance protocol, exec altitude)

```
Subject: Headcount Data

Priya,

Series D plus three acquisitions.
Board reporting on headcount just got harder.

When people data sits in PeopleNest plus spreadsheets, finance rebuilds the numbers by hand.
Teams at your stage lose 10 or more hours a month there.

A 1,200 person robotics firm cut that to one live view.

Open to seeing the math?

PS. If someone on your team runs people systems day to day, happy to connect with them directly.
```

**Why it's built this way:**

- **Router:** Finance department → Finance protocol supreme. Everything shifts to **cost, time, and risk** — no "people and culture" language, which would read as noise to this buyer.
- **The logic chain, not the logo:** the proof customer is framed by the problem mapping ("cut that to one live view"), because finance buyers discount name-drops without the reasoning. Note it's anonymized here — the finance protocol prefers the mapping over the brand when the industry match is loose.
- **Quantified, but restrained:** one contextualized number. Finance is trained to distrust stacked or too-good stats; one beats three.
- **Measured tone:** no superlatives, no exclamation points, no hype — the finance landmine list.
- **Two-path CTA:** a low-friction ask plus a PS delegate route — the pattern for finance executives.
- **Craft law:** 53 words before the PS. Subject 2 words, boring on purpose. Mobile-formatted. Zero em dashes.

## What to notice across the pair

Same account. Same three triggers. Same underlying product. And yet: different subject, different opening logic, different vocabulary, different proof treatment, different CTA mechanics — because **exactly one protocol governs each message**, and the protocols encode how differently these two buyers read cold email. This is the "supreme law" model working: the system cannot produce a one-size email for two seats, because no single skill has jurisdiction over both.

Both drafts passed two gates before delivery: the universal 11-point craft grade, then the persona protocol's own checklist. In production, a draft that cites any number not present in the verified-stats file fails the final checkbox and gets rewritten — fabricated proof is a structural impossibility, not a discouraged behavior.
