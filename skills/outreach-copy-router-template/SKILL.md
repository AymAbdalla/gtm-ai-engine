# Copy Engine — Craft Law + Persona Router

This is the foundation under every cold email and DM. Two jobs:

1. **Route** every message to the **persona-matched protocol that is SUPREME** for that buyer.
2. **Supply the universal craft law** all protocols inherit — true no matter who you're emailing.

> **Source of authority:** published benchmark research, credited above. Never invent a stat. Customer proof comes only from the evidence-bank skill.

---

## The Persona Router (run FIRST, before drafting or grading)

**Step 1 — identify the recipient's DEPARTMENT.** The department-matched protocol becomes **SUPREME LAW** for that copy. It outranks this core wherever they differ on tone, altitude, or proof.

| Department | Supreme protocol | Why it's different (benchmark) |
|---|---|---|
| HR / People / Talent | HR protocol (embedded below) | Rewards warmth; punishes templates; 3.4% baseline reply |
| Finance | Finance protocol | Lowest A-rate anywhere (6.1%) but biggest A-grade lift (+79%) — quality matters most |
| IT / Security / Technical | IT protocol | Highest baseline (5.2%), smallest A-lift — credibility beats polish; name-drops hurt |
| Operations | Ops protocol | Punishes presumptive openers — turn the assumption into a question |

**Step 2 — identify SENIORITY** (Exec / Director / Manager / IC) and match the **altitude** inside the chosen protocol: execs get company-level strategy and a delegate path; directors get one use case with proof; managers get one workflow; ICs get the shortest, warmest notes.

**Routing rules:**
- **One protocol governs each message.** Never let the HR protocol govern a CFO email. Same account, different seat = different supreme law, different subject, different body.
- **No department match** (Legal, Marketing…) → core craft + the nearest protocol's altitude logic.
- **Multi-threading one account:** every seat's message must independently pass its own protocol. No shared subject line, no copy-paste body.

## The Universal Craft Law (every message, every persona)

Ten rules, each traceable to a benchmark stat:

1. **Short: 25–50 words** (ceiling ~75). Short mobile-optimized emails lift replies +83%. Readers scan ~9–11 seconds. *(Openers only — follow-ups play by different rules.)*
2. **One idea per email, paragraph, and sentence.** Drop the commas that conjoin ideas.
3. **Write at a 3rd–5th grade reading level** (+67% replies). Most cold email is written at 10th grade or above; credibility comes from understanding their problem, not from jargon.
4. **Mobile-first formatting.** First opens are far more likely on a phone. One sentence per line. One desktop line = 2–4 phone lines.
5. **Build curiosity; don't inform.** Informative tone inversely correlates with replies. Give only the 6-second answer to "what is it?"
6. **Lead with the buyer's problem, never your product.** Personalization must connect to the problem you solve — not where they went to school.
7. **Framework, not template:** Observation → Problem/Insight → Credibility → one line of "what we do" → CTC. Every line earns the next.
8. **CTC over CTA.** The only goal is a reply. Never ask for the meeting — a calendar ask trips the mental spam filter. One easy question, low friction. Zero or one question total.
9. **Subject line: 1–3 words (2 optimal), boring, internal-sounding, title case.** The preview text — the first two body sentences — is the real open driver.
10. **Sound human.** Kill clichés and hype; lower the formality; read it aloud. **No em dashes and no "--" anywhere in send copy** — they read as machine-written.

### The anti-AI-tell layer

Hard-banned in send copy: em/en dashes, double hyphens, trailing ellipses, semicolons, and the AI-tell vocabulary (`delve · leverage · utilize · streamline · robust · seamless · elevate · unlock · empower · game-changer · "I hope this email finds you well"` …). Final test: **a sharp peer would actually type this.**

## Universal Self-Grade (run BEFORE the persona checklist)

1. ☐ Router run — correct supreme protocol, altitude matched to seniority?
2. ☐ Under ~50 words? One idea per line?
3. ☐ 3rd–5th grade reading level, no jargon-for-credibility?
4. ☐ Formats cleanly on a phone?
5. ☐ Opens on THEM, not on you or your product?
6. ☐ Builds curiosity rather than explaining the product?
7. ☐ Every line logically earns the next?
8. ☐ Ends on ONE low-friction CTC, not a meeting ask?
9. ☐ Subject 1–3 words, internal-looking; first two lines work as preview?
10. ☐ Human tone; no banned punctuation or AI-tell words?
11. ☐ Any stat or customer traces to the evidence bank? (Zero invented numbers.)

Any box unchecked → rewrite before the persona protocol's own checklist.

# EMBEDDED EXAMPLE — The HR Persona Protocol (SUPREME for HR buyers)

*In production this is its own skill file. It's embedded here so the template shows a complete protocol. Its structure — throughline, altitude playbook per tier, self-grade checklist, evidence rules — is the pattern every department protocol follows.*

## The throughline (memorize this)

HR rewards **warmth** and punishes **templates**. Tone stays warm at every level; what changes is the **altitude** of the problem you're solving. Match the warmth they expect. Match the altitude to the seniority. Prove you understand their world, not just their title.

## Persona altitude playbook (identify seniority before drafting)

**1. Executives (CHRO, CPO, VP People, Head of People)** — strategic altitude. Lead with a company-level observation (funding, headcount milestone, expansion) connected to a people challenge typical of their stage. Frame outcomes people-first, not product-first. Keep it short and human. **Always include a delegate path** ("if someone on your team is closer to this…") — executives love to route.

**2. Directors & senior leaders** — functional altitude; the benchmark's underserved tier (baseline 3.4% → 8.4% on an A-grade, the biggest absolute lift in the dataset). Anchor on **ONE program they own** with one proof from a similar-stage company. Never pitch "transform your function" — they disengage instantly.

**3. Managers** — tactical altitude. One specific workflow they run every week; the question they're really asking is "does this make my Tuesday easier?" Tone: helpful colleague who gets it.

**4. Individual contributors** — the most responsive tier on A-grade copy. **Shortest and warmest emails of any tier.** Name the exact task eating their afternoon; tangible immediate outcome; low-stakes ask. Never ROI or strategy — they don't hold budget, and the goal is a conversation or a route to the owner.

## Voice standard

- Subjects sound like an internal colleague's email: situational, plain, 2–3 words. Never a blog headline, never clever.
- Open with a discovery question or a plain observation. Describe the **actual sequential steps of the pain** — the real steps they live through weekly — not the concept of the pain.
- The product is introduced **once, plainly**: "X connects A to B automatically." Never "our industry-leading platform enables seamless…"
- One proof line, dropped naturally. CTA is 4–6 words.

## Evidence rules (the anti-fabrication gate)

```
[CONFIGURE — point these at your own verified evidence bank:]
```

- Every number must trace to the **verified-stats file**. Every customer name must appear in the **evidence database**. No invented stats, no vague "companies like yours saw…" without a named source. **Zero exceptions.**
- **Band-match or anonymize:** same industry + same size band → name the customer. Same band, different industry → anonymize to "a [industry] customer at your size." No match → use the one approved generic proof line, or omit proof entirely. **Never name a smaller-band customer to a larger prospect. Never invent.**
- One proof point per touch. Lead with the pain, not the case study.

## HR self-grade checklist (after the universal grade)

1. ☐ Tier identified and altitude matched?
2. ☐ Tone warm and human — would an HR person feel this was written *to them*?
3. ☐ Specific, observable opener?
4. ☐ Problem in people-first language, not product language?
5. ☐ ONE value prop, framed as benefit?
6. ☐ CTA specific, simple, low-stakes?
7. ☐ Execs: delegate option included? Directors: ONE use case with proof? Managers: a specific workflow? ICs: shortest + warmest?
8. ☐ Avoided: "transform", "on your radar", "quick chat", "touch base", feature dumps?
9. ☐ Every stat and customer traced to the evidence bank?
10. ☐ Would this earn a top-decile grade?

# Building the sibling protocols

Each department protocol follows the same skeleton: **(1)** the benchmark numbers for that department and why they demand a different approach, **(2)** a one-line throughline, **(3)** the department's landmine list (what instantly kills credibility with this buyer), **(4)** the four-tier altitude playbook with do/don't per tier, **(5)** the department-specific self-grade checklist, **(6)** evidence rules pointing at the shared bank.

The department differences that matter (from the published benchmarks):

- **Finance:** thinks in cost, time, and risk. The logic chain matters more than the logo — a name-drop means nothing without the mapping ("their problem was X, here's how it maps to yours"). No superlatives, no exclamation points, no stacked claims. Trained to distrust numbers that sound too good: one contextualized proof beats three impressive ones.
- **IT:** authenticity over polish. Hype words ("world-class," "cutting-edge") are instant credibility killers; name-drops actively hurt. Frame everything as risk reduced or velocity gained.
- **Operations:** never open with an assumption about their world — turn the hypothesis into a question ("How are you handling X as you scale?"). Name a specific process, never a category.

## Litmus test (craft level — each protocol adds its own)

If a draft could be sent to any buyer in any department unchanged → it **fails** the router.
If it reads like a sequence tool wrote it → it **fails**.
If it's over ~75 words or wall-formatted on a phone → it **fails**.
If it asks for the meeting instead of starting a conversation → it **fails**.
If it contains an em dash or "--" → it **fails**.
